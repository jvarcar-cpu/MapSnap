## CHRONICLE-MSN-0001 — The First Public Presence

**Date:** 2026-07-11  
**Type:** Foundational Product Chronicle  
**Status:** Ratified — Permanent — do not modify

### Summary

On 11 July 2026, MapSnap became publicly available on the Internet under its own domain:

https://mapsnap.se

This event marked the transition from an internal prototype to a publicly accessible product.

Although technically modest, this milestone represents the completion of MapSnap's first product lifecycle:

Observation → Idea → Identity → Product → Public Presence

It also became the first independently developed product in this emerging product lineage to complete the full journey from concept to continuous deployment on its own public domain.

### Background

MapSnap did not begin as a startup idea.

It began during real work.

While driving taxi on Gotland, countless interesting locations appeared unexpectedly.

Beautiful beaches.

Small harbors.

Viewpoints.

Historic places.

Restaurants.

Hidden roads.

There was rarely enough time to stop.

The next customer had already arrived.

The place disappeared.

The problem was never navigation.

The problem was memory.

The world was passing by faster than it could be remembered.

The insight became simple:

"Capture the place before it disappears."

This sentence later became the identity of the product.

### Discovery

The first product discussions were not about technology.

They were about behavior.

How quickly can a place be saved?

What interrupts the user?

What happens if there is no signal?

What if both hands are occupied?

Those discussions slowly formed the first product doctrines.

Among them:

- One Second Rule
- Thumb First
- Reality First
- Offline First
- Snap Principle

The architecture emerged from the experience rather than the technology.

### Building the Product

The product was intentionally kept small.

Every feature had to justify its existence.

The first working prototype introduced:

- instant location capture
- camera integration
- offline storage
- local persistence
- backup and restore
- minimal interface
- one central action

The application deliberately resisted feature creep.

The objective was not to become another mapping application.

The objective was to remember reality.

### First Validation

The first validation did not come from analytics.

It came from everyday use.

MapSnap was used while exploring Visby.

Later, it helped locate a parked vehicle after wandering through the old town.

That moment quietly demonstrated that the product solved a real problem.

No survey was needed.

Reality had answered.

### Identity

As development continued, MapSnap became more than software.

It became a product with its own identity.

Its purpose was summarized as:

"Capture places before they disappear."

The product was intentionally calm.

Minimal.

Honest.

It avoided unnecessary complexity.

The interface was designed to disappear behind the experience.

### Public Presence

The final transition required several operational milestones.

The project received:

- Git repository
- GitHub repository
- continuous deployment
- Vercel hosting
- production deployment
- HTTPS
- public domain
- DNS integration

After successful deployment and DNS propagation, MapSnap became available under:

https://mapsnap.se

For the first time, the product no longer existed only on the developer's computer.

It existed on the public Internet.

### Historical Significance

This Chronicle does not celebrate the deployment itself.

Deployments happen every day.

It records something more important.

The moment an idea became a product that anyone could visit.

It also established an institutional pattern:

A product reaches historical maturity not when it is finished, but when it becomes publicly reachable.

Future products may be larger.

They may serve millions of users.

But MapSnap will remain the first product in this lineage to cross that threshold.

### Relationship to PDE

MapSnap occupies a unique historical position.

It was conceived before PDE Foundation reached operational maturity.

It was therefore not created by PDE.

Instead, it developed independently.

Ironically, this independence later revealed an important architectural insight.

The journey of bringing an existing project into the PDE ecosystem represents a distinct lifecycle that had not yet been formally defined.

MapSnap therefore becomes the reference case for a possible future PDE capability:

Product Onboarding Lifecycle

This future process may define how externally created software becomes an institutional PDE product without rewriting its history.

This is an architectural discovery, not yet a ratified PDE decision.

### Legacy

Products often begin with ambitious business plans.

MapSnap began with a taxi driver who repeatedly lost beautiful places because work required moving on.

That simple observation became software.

That software became a product.

That product became public.

Everything that follows begins here.

### Institutional Closing Statement

11 July 2026

MapSnap crossed the boundary between development and existence.

From this day forward,

MapSnap belongs not only to its creator, but to the world.
